What we found using TxLINE/TxODDS to build a match-integrity detector

FairWhistle watches odds microstructure for repricing patterns public information can't explain, using the TxLINE/TxODDS free tier as its only data source — a live World Cup Final fixture, a 105-fixture historical sweep, and a full day of live polling. This is the feedback the team already shared in-thread, written up properly: what worked, what tripped us up, and the one finding worth a design note of its own.

fixture 18257739 · FIFA World Cup Final — Spain v Argentina · 2026-07-19

What worked well

The free tier is genuinely real, rich data — not a toy sandbox. /api/odds/updates/{fixtureId} returns full match history (60k+ rows for one fixture), and it worked identically for a live in-progress fixture and for historical fixtures pulled from a separate archive used to cross-check the live path. We hit zero rate limits across the full sweep plus a full day of live polling.

Gotchas we found the hard way

No docs pointed at any of these — all discovered by trial and error while wiring the live and backtest paths.

Odds snapshots silently return empty without asOf

/api/odds/snapshot/{fixtureId} returns nothing — no error, just an empty result — unless you pass ?asOf=<now-ms>.

GET /api/odds/snapshot/18257739                    -> []
GET /api/odds/snapshot/18257739?asOf=1753142400000 -> [ ...rows ]

Prices are decimal odds ×1000

Undocumented, and an easy footgun if you render a raw Prices value as-is. Divide by 1000 to get the decimal odds you actually want.

odds: row.Prices[i] / 1000

/api/scores/updates/{fixtureId} doesn't work — use the snapshot endpoint

Use /api/scores/snapshot/{fixtureId} instead. It returns a growing list of events rather than a single current state, so you have to take the max-Seq row yourself to know what "now" actually is.

MarketPeriod needs an explicit null filter

Half-specific rows (MarketPeriod = half=1, etc.) sit in the same stream, under the same SuperOddsType, as full-match rows. Without row.MarketPeriod !== null you'll silently mix half-time and full-match markets together.

PriceNames / MarketParameters conventions took trial-and-error

Outcome naming isn't consistent across market types — part1/draw/part2 for 1X2, but over/under for totals — and line selection is a string to match (MarketParameters === "line=2.5"), not a structured field.

The centerpiece finding: the scores feed lags the odds feed

An undocumented, inconsistent lag — and it's the whole story behind today's live alert

On the free tier, the scores/action feed lags the odds feed by a meaningfully long and inconsistent window. This isn't documented anywhere as expected behavior, and we only found it because it's exactly what happened on today's live alert on /story.html: a real detector fired on a real price move with zero explanation visible in the data at that moment, and only ~20–40 minutes later did the score feed catch up and reveal the yellow card and shot sequence that had explained the move the whole time.

That gap isn't a bug in our detector — it's a property of the free-tier scores feed that any consumer building on real-time odds + scores together needs to plan for, and right now the only way to learn about it is to get flagged by it.

Recommendations

01

A short "gotchas" doc covering the three sharpest edges: the asOf requirement on odds snapshots, the ×1000 price scaling, and which scores endpoint is actually live (snapshot, not updates).

02

Publish the typical odds-vs-scores feed lag — even a rough distribution would turn what we discovered empirically today into documented, designed-around behavior instead of a surprise downstream consumers have to hit for themselves first.

An official SDK would have saved real time today

An official SDK — similar to 0xPulsePlay/txline-kit (demo) — would genuinely have saved real time today. The team independently rediscovered several conventions a kit like that already encodes: the asOf requirement, the price scaling, the live scores endpoint, and the MarketPeriod/PriceNames shape — all of which cost us trial-and-error hours a maintained client would have skipped entirely.
